Evidence infrastructure requires dedicated investment
There was broad agreement across implementers, researchers, and donors that generating rigorous evidence without diverting funds from humanitarian action requires ring-fenced financing. Evaluation design and data systems are infrastructure, not overhead.
"Evaluation design, data systems, joint research — these are not overhead. They are infrastructure. Donors who invest in the evidence ecosystem spend smarter, and those who incentivise agencies to evaluate together get faster learning at a fraction of the cost."
— Antonio Scognamillo, FAO ESA · What's next? Interactive session
Pre-agreed rules can only be grounded in accumulated evidence
Speakers consistently noted that anticipatory action's defining feature — pre-agreed plans, triggers, and finance — is only credible when grounded in what the community has already learned. Evidence is therefore not optional but constitutive of the model.
"Pre-agreed plans, pre-agreed decisions, pre-arranged finance — these are the backbone of anticipatory action, and what makes evidence so important: to have such pre-agreed rules, we need to ground them on what we have already learned."
— Niccolo Lombardi, FAO OER · Setting the stage
Use existing evidence before commissioning new studies
A recurring view was that the community does not always need more research — it needs to synthesise and act on what already exists. New evidence generation should be reserved for genuine gaps where existing signals are insufficient for decision-making.
"If the evidence exists, do a synthesis and use the best that is out there — it does not have to be experimental. If it is signalling enough for you to make a decision, run with it. But if you do not have it, use the opportunity to build that evidence base."
— Dean Karlan, IPA · Aligning evidence and action: reflections from a donor, implementer, and researcher
Concrete commitments with owners and timelines are the next step
The closing session coalesced around three deliverables that should follow the workshop: a shared research agenda, joint publications, and a light coordination mechanism. The emphasis was on named owners and realistic timelines rather than aspirational declarations.
"Three concrete commitments should come out of these two days: a shared research agenda, joint evidence-based publications, and a light coordination mechanism to keep this collaboration alive — with owners and timelines."
— Maximo Torero, FAO Chief Economist · Closing remarks and way forward
Open data reduces duplication and strengthens collective evidence
Multiple participants noted that organisations routinely collect the same data in the same contexts for slightly different purposes. Anonymising and sharing datasets across agencies was identified as a high-impact, near-term action to reduce waste and enable joint evaluation.
"Making data available and anonymising it would allow reduction of overlapping and duplication of effort — collecting the same type of data in the same country but in a slightly different time or for a slightly different learning objective adds little value."
— Romina Cavatassi, World Bank · Breaking down silos
A common language for outcomes is a prerequisite for comparison
Without agreed outcome variables and shared definitions, findings from different organisations remain impossible to synthesise or compare across contexts. Harmonising on a core set of indicators was identified as an immediate and tractable first step.
"We need to coordinate, speak the same language, define our outcome variables and the gaps we want to deepen — even a shared file that captures who is studying what and where is already a meaningful step."
— Participant · What's next? Interactive session
Inter-agency collaboration is already advanced — build on it
Participants cautioned against framing coordination as something that needs to be invented from scratch. Existing forums, shared frameworks, and joint working groups already exist and are functioning. The task is deepening and formalising what works, not starting over.
"There is already a lot of good collaboration under way. We should build on it — not start from scratch — and extend it into the evidence space."
— Clemens Gros, Climate Centre · Breaking down silos
Evidence must be both rigorous and decision-relevant
A consistent theme across both days was that rigour alone is insufficient. Evidence that is methodologically sound but arrives too late or addresses the wrong questions does not change decisions. The community must pursue both dimensions simultaneously.
"We need to generate evidence that is credible and compelling — rigorous enough that people cannot dismiss it with competing explanations, but also decision-relevant so it changes what we actually do."
— Karen Macours, PSE · Keynote: evaluating what matters
